\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}

\subsection{Setup}

\paragraph{Model and training.}
We use Qwen3.5-9B \citep{qwen2025} with LoRA ($r{=}64$, $\alpha{=}128$) on the GSM8K training set \citep{cobbe2021gsm8k}.
Training uses GRPO with group size $G{=}4$, binary answer-verification reward, learning rate $2{\times}10^{-5}$, gradient accumulation 4, and 200 optimization steps.
All variants share identical model, dataset, hyperparameters, and LoRA configuration; only the advantage formulation and replay mechanism differ.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
We evaluate on the \emph{full} GSM8K test set ($n{=}1{,}319$ questions) using greedy decoding and exact answer matching.
We report accuracy over 3 or 4 random seeds (42, 43, 44, and optionally 45) with mean and standard deviation.

\paragraph{Baselines and ablations.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Base model}: Qwen3.5-9B without any RL training.
\item \textbf{Dr.GRPO}: Group-mean-centered advantage with Dr.GRPO's length-bias correction \citep{liu2025understanding}. 4 seeds.
\item \textbf{TASA-only} (B): Threshold-anchored signed advantage without replay. 3 seeds.
\item \textbf{SPO+Fixed Replay} (A): Per-prompt EMA baseline advantage with fixed-$\lambda$ uniform CE replay---a legacy method. 3 seeds.
\item \textbf{TASA+CE Replay} (D): Our full method. TASA advantage plus verified CE replay ($\lambda{=}0.05$, warmup $W{=}50$ steps, bank capacity $K{=}2$ per prompt). 3 seeds.
\item \textbf{TASA+Contrastive Pair} (C): TASA with a DPO-style pairwise log-sigmoid replay loss over prompt-local verified success/failure pairs, instead of positive-only CE replay. 3 seeds.
\end{itemize}

\Cref{tab:main} presents the main results.
Three findings stand out:

\paragraph{(1) Dr.GRPO fails under binary reward at $G{=}4$.}
Dr.GRPO achieves only 27.8\%, barely above the base model's 25.5\%.
This confirms the binary degeneracy: with $G{=}4$ and rewards in $\{0,1\}$, a large fraction of groups are homogeneous, producing zero advantage and no gradient.

\paragraph{(2) TASA restores effective learning.}
TASA-only (B) achieves 46.8\%, a +19.0~pp improvement over Dr.GRPO.
In a separate 4-seed experiment, TASA reaches 62.6\% mean (with seeds 56.1\%, 73.6\%, 67.3\%, 53.3\%), confirming the large and robust advantage of threshold anchoring.

\paragraph{(3) CE replay adds +8.3~pp on top of TASA.}
The full method (D) achieves 55.0\%, an additional +8.3~pp over TASA-only.
The replay bank allows the model to revisit verified solutions from prompts it encountered earlier, reinforcing correct reasoning patterns.

\subsection{Ablation: Contrastive Pair vs.\ Positive CE Replay}
\label{sec:ablation}

\Cref{tab:main} shows that TASA+Contrastive Pair (C) achieves 49.0\%, which is +2.2~pp better than TASA-only but 6.0~pp \emph{worse} than TASA+CE Replay (D).
This is notable because the contrastive variant has access to both successes and failures (unlike D, which uses only successes), yet it performs worse.

We hypothesize that under sparse binary reward, incorrect responses often contain valid intermediate reasoning steps.
The pairwise negative signal penalizes the entire completion, indiscriminately suppressing both erroneous and useful tokens.
Positive CE replay avoids this by only reinforcing verified-correct solutions without penalizing any specific failure.

An additional observation is that contrastive pair replay has the \emph{lowest} variance among the non-trivial methods (std = 2.1 vs.\ 7.0 for D and 3.4 for B), suggesting that negative contrast stabilizes training but at the cost of lower mean performance.
