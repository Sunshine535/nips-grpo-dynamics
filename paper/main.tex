\begin{abstract}
Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) is the dominant reinforcement learning algorithm for post-training large language models on reasoning tasks, yet practitioners lack principled guidance for configuring the positive/negative signal balance.
We present the first explicit signal-balance stability map for GRPO under binary verifiable rewards.
By introducing the effective balance ratio $\rho$ as a single control variable, we derive a reduced-model stability analysis that predicts three training regimes---convergent, gradient-starved, and unstable---as a function of $\rho$, the zero-group rate $p_0$, group size $G$, and standard GRPO hyperparameters.
Our analysis yields: (i) a sharp lower bound $\rhomin$ below which gradient starvation occurs (Theorem~\ref{thm:starvation}), (ii) an approximate upper bound $\rhomax$ beyond which training destabilizes (Proposition~\ref{prop:instability}), and (iii) AdaBalance, a minimum-variance adaptive controller derived as a direct corollary with only two hyperparameters.
Experiments on Qwen3.5-9B/GSM8K validate the theoretical boundaries against empirical training outcomes across a grid of $\rho$ values, and AdaBalance is evaluated against vanilla GRPO, oracle best-static $\rho$, a linear scheduler, and GTPO-style baselines.
The contribution is a geometry-of-optimization result, not a new algorithm.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards has become the standard approach for improving the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs)~\citep{deepseekr1,qwen3}.
Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)~\citep{shao2024deepseekmath} is the dominant algorithm in this paradigm: for each prompt, GRPO generates a group of $G$ completions, normalizes their binary rewards into advantages, and updates the policy via a clipped surrogate objective.
Despite its widespread adoption---DeepSeek-R1, Qwen3, and numerous open-source reasoning models all rely on GRPO---the algorithm's sensitivity to the positive/negative signal balance remains poorly understood.

In practice, GRPO training is fragile.
When the model is weak (low success rate $p$), most groups receive all-zero rewards, producing zero advantages and \emph{gradient starvation}.
When positive signals are over-weighted, the policy diverges from the reference model, causing \emph{KL blow-up} and reward hacking.
Existing fixes address these symptoms independently: GTPO~\citep{gtpo} skips zero-score groups, DaGRPO~\citep{dagrpo} masks within-group pairs, and TR-GRPO~\citep{trgrpo} applies token-level reweighting.
None provides a unifying characterization of \emph{when} and \emph{why} these failure modes arise.

We fill this gap by analyzing the stability landscape of GRPO through a single lens: the \textbf{effective balance ratio} $\rho$.
Our key insight is that the diverse ad-hoc modifications proposed in the literature can be understood as implicitly adjusting $\rho$, and that the training dynamics are governed by the interaction between $\rho$ and the zero-group starvation rate $\GSR(p, G) = (1-p)^G + p^G$.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Stability analysis} (Theorems~\ref{thm:degenerate}--\ref{thm:starvation}, Proposition~\ref{prop:instability}): Under three explicit assumptions on binary verifiable rewards, we derive a gradient variance decomposition for the $\rho$-weighted GRPO objective and characterize three regimes---convergent, gradient-starved, and unstable---with computable boundaries that depend only on observable training statistics.
\item \textbf{AdaBalance controller} (Corollary~\ref{cor:adabalance}): The minimum-variance $\rhostar$ within the $\{\Lrho : \rho > 0\}$ objective family, estimable online at $O(G)$ cost per group, with two hyperparameters ($K{=}50$, $\tau{=}0.1$) that have principled defaults.
\item \textbf{Empirical validation}: On Qwen3.5-9B/GSM8K, we evaluate the theoretical boundaries against empirical training outcomes across a 9-point $\rho$ sweep with multiple seeds. AdaBalance is compared against vanilla GRPO, oracle best-static $\rho$, linear scheduling, and GTPO-style baselines.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{GRPO and variants.}
GRPO~\citep{shao2024deepseekmath} replaces PPO's value function with group-relative normalization, reducing memory and compute cost for LLM post-training.
GTPO~\citep{gtpo} identifies the zero-score problem and proposes skipping all-zero groups.
DaGRPO~\citep{dagrpo} masks within-group advantage pairs to reduce noise.
TR-GRPO~\citep{trgrpo} applies token-level reward redistribution.
These are ad-hoc engineering fixes; we provide the theoretical foundation that explains when each is needed.

\paragraph{RL post-training for reasoning.}
DeepSeek-R1~\citep{deepseekr1} demonstrated that GRPO with binary verifiable rewards can train chain-of-thought reasoning without supervised fine-tuning.
Qwen3~\citep{qwen3} extended this approach to larger scales.
\citet{mroueh2025} derived GRPO dynamics and success amplification but did not address the signal balance dimension.
\citet{zhou2026} showed GRPO's gradient is a U-statistic and derived optimal group size, but their analysis is orthogonal to ours.

\paragraph{Policy gradient variance reduction.}
The variance of policy gradient estimators has been studied extensively in the RL literature~\citep{schulman2016gae,tucker2018mirage}.
Our work contributes a domain-specific variance decomposition for GRPO under binary rewards, where the binomial structure of group outcomes enables closed-form analysis.

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{GRPO setup.}
For a prompt $x$ drawn from dataset $\mathcal{D}$, GRPO generates a group of $G$ completions $\{y_1, \ldots, y_G\}$ from policy $\pitheta$.
Each completion receives a binary reward $r_i \in \{0, 1\}$ from a verifier.
The group success count is $m = \sum_{i=1}^G r_i$.

Standard GRPO normalizes rewards into advantages:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:grpo-advantage}
a_i(m) = \frac{r_i - m/G}{\max(\sigma(m), \delta)}, \quad \sigma(m) = \frac{\sqrt{m(G-m)}}{G}, \quad \delta = \frac{1}{G}.
\end{equation}
The GRPO objective combines a clipped surrogate with a KL penalty:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:grpo-objective}
L(\btheta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}} \mathbb{E}_{m \sim \Bin(G, p(x))} \left[\sum_{i=1}^G \min\!\big(r_\btheta \cdot a_i,\; \clip(r_\btheta, 1{-}\epsilon, 1{+}\epsilon) \cdot a_i\big)\right] + \lambda_{\mathrm{KL}} \cdot \mathrm{KL}(\pitheta \| \piref),
\end{equation}
where $r_\btheta = \pitheta(y_i|x) / \piref(y_i|x)$ is the importance ratio.

\section{Stability Analysis of $\rho$-Weighted GRPO}
\label{sec:method}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Assumptions}

\begin{assumption}[Binary verifiable rewards]
\label{asm:binary}
$r_i \in \{0, 1\}$ for all completions.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Group i.i.d.]
\label{asm:iid}
Within a group for prompt $x$, rewards are i.i.d.\ $\Ber(p(x))$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Sufficient statistic]
\label{asm:sufficient}
The per-prompt success probability $p(x)$ is the sufficient statistic for the reward distribution.
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}
Assumption~\ref{asm:iid} is the strongest; it approximately holds when completions are sampled independently and the verifier is deterministic.
We test robustness to its violation in Section~\ref{sec:exp-robustness}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The $\rho$-Weighted Objective Family}

We introduce the effective balance ratio $\rho > 0$ that rescales positive advantages \emph{after} group normalization but \emph{before} the clipped surrogate:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rho-advantage}
\tilde{a}_i(m) = \begin{cases}
\rho \cdot a_i(m) & \text{if } r_i = 1, \\
a_i(m) & \text{if } r_i = 0.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The modified objective is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rho-objective}
\Lrho(\btheta) = \mathbb{E}_x \mathbb{E}_m \left[\sum_{i=1}^G \min\!\big(r_\btheta \cdot \tilde{a}_i,\; \clip(r_\btheta, 1{-}\epsilon, 1{+}\epsilon) \cdot \tilde{a}_i\big)\right] + \lambda_{\mathrm{KL}} \cdot \mathrm{KL}(\pitheta \| \piref).
\end{equation}
Setting $\rho = 1$ recovers standard GRPO.
The family $\{\Lrho : \rho > 0\}$ parameterizes a continuum of objectives from pure negative signal ($\rho \to 0$) to pure positive signal ($\rho \to \infty$).

\subsection{Theoretical Results}

Under Assumptions~\ref{asm:binary}--\ref{asm:sufficient}, $m \sim \Bin(G, p(x))$.

\begin{theorem}[Degenerate group starvation]
\label{thm:degenerate}
For $m = 0$ or $m = G$, $a_i(m) = 0$ for all $i \in \{1, \ldots, G\}$.
These degenerate groups contribute zero gradient regardless of $\rho$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
When $m = 0$: $r_i = 0$ for all $i$, so $a_i = (0 - 0)/\delta = 0$.
When $m = G$: $r_i = 1$ for all $i$, so $a_i = (1 - 1)/\delta = 0$.
\end{proof}

The fraction of degenerate groups defines the \textbf{group starvation rate}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gsr}
\GSR(p, G) = P(m=0) + P(m=G) = (1-p)^G + p^G.
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Gradient variance decomposition]
\label{thm:variance}
Under Assumptions~\ref{asm:binary}--\ref{asm:sufficient}, the variance of the $\rho$-weighted GRPO gradient estimator for a single prompt $x$ with success probability $p = p(x)$ decomposes as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:variance-decomposition}
\Var(\widehat{\nabla} \Lrho \mid x) = \rho^2 \cdot V_+(p, G) + V_-(p, G) + 2\rho \cdot C(p, G),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{align}
V_+(p, G) &= \sum_{m=1}^{G-1} \binom{G}{m} p^m (1{-}p)^{G-m} \cdot m \cdot a_+(m)^2 \cdot \sigma^2_{\nabla,+}, \label{eq:Vplus} \\
V_-(p, G) &= \sum_{m=1}^{G-1} \binom{G}{m} p^m (1{-}p)^{G-m} \cdot (G{-}m) \cdot a_-(m)^2 \cdot \sigma^2_{\nabla,-}, \label{eq:Vminus} \\
C(p, G) &= \frac{1}{G}\sum_{m=1}^{G-1} \binom{G}{m} p^m (1{-}p)^{G-m} \cdot [m \cdot a_+(m)] \cdot [(G{-}m) \cdot a_-(m)], \label{eq:CpG}
\end{align}
with $a_+(m) = (1 - m/G)/\sigma(m)$, $a_-(m) = (0 - m/G)/\sigma(m)$.
$V_+$ and $V_-$ include per-sample gradient variance scaling ($\sigma^2_{\nabla,+}$, $\sigma^2_{\nabla,-}$, default to 1 when unavailable).
$C$ captures the structural coupling between positive and negative advantage contributions through the shared group outcome $m$; it is always negative under binary rewards and does not require separate gradient covariance estimation.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Conditioning on the group outcome $m$ and using the law of total variance, the gradient of $\Lrho$ decomposes into contributions from $m$ positive and $(G-m)$ negative samples.
By Assumption~\ref{asm:iid}, samples within a group are independent, so cross-sample variance terms vanish within each reward class.
The $\rho$-weighting applies to positive samples, giving the quadratic form in $\rho$.
Full derivation in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Lower stability bound---gradient starvation]
\label{thm:starvation}
Training suffers gradient starvation when positive gradient signal is overwhelmed by negative variance.
The minimum $\rho$ to maintain positive signal is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rho-min}
\rhomin(p, G) = \frac{V_-(p, G)}{2|C(p, G)|} \quad \text{when } C(p, G) < 0.
\end{equation}
This bound depends only on $p$, $G$, and gradient statistics---no extra hyperparameters.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
From Eq.~\eqref{eq:variance-decomposition}, the gradient is dominated by noise when the positive-signal contribution $\rho^2 V_+$ is insufficient to overcome negative-signal variance $V_-$.
Setting the derivative $\partial \Var / \partial \rho = 2\rho V_+ + 2C = 0$ and solving for the minimum-variance $\rho$ gives the balance point.
Below this, positive gradient signal is starved.
The condition $C < 0$ holds when positive and negative gradient directions are anti-correlated, which is the typical case in GRPO (correct and incorrect completions push the policy in opposing directions).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Upper instability bound---approximate]
\label{prop:instability}
Training becomes unstable when $\rho$ exceeds:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rho-max}
\rhomax(p, G, \lambda_{\mathrm{KL}}, \epsilon) \approx \frac{1}{\epsilon} \cdot \frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{KL}}}{\|\nabla_+\|} \cdot \sigma(\bar{m}),
\end{equation}
where $\bar{m} = pG$ is the expected success count and $\|\nabla_+\|$ is the mean positive gradient norm.
This upper bound additionally depends on $\lambda_{\mathrm{KL}}$ and $\epsilon$; it is an empirically-calibrated guideline, not a sharp theorem.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}
Proposition~\ref{prop:instability} captures the intuition that large $\rho$ amplifies positive advantages beyond the clipping threshold $\epsilon$, overwhelming the KL regularization.
The approximation is tightest when the policy is close to the reference ($r_\btheta \approx 1$).
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Stability Map}

Combining Theorems~\ref{thm:degenerate}--\ref{thm:starvation} and Proposition~\ref{prop:instability}, the training landscape partitions into three regimes in the $(\rho, p_0)$ plane:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Convergent}: $\rho \in [\rhomin, \rhomax]$ with moderate $p_0$. The gradient has sufficient signal in both directions with bounded variance.
\item \textbf{Gradient-starved}: $\rho < \rhomin$ or $\GSR$ exceeds threshold $\tau_\star$. Degenerate groups dominate, learning stalls.
\item \textbf{Unstable}: $\rho > \rhomax$. Positive signal overwhelms KL regularization, causing divergence.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{AdaBalance: Minimum-Variance Adaptive Controller}

\begin{corollary}[AdaBalance]
\label{cor:adabalance}
The $\rho$ that minimizes $\Var(\widehat{\nabla} \Lrho)$ within the $\{\Lrho : \rho > 0\}$ family is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rho-star}
\rhostar = \frac{-C(p, G)}{V_+(p, G)},
\end{equation}
estimable online from the success-count histogram at cost $O(G)$ per group.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Setting $\partial \Var / \partial \rho = 2\rho V_+ + 2C = 0$ and solving: $\rhostar = -C/V_+$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
$\rhostar$ minimizes variance of the \emph{modified} objective $\Lrho$, not the original GRPO objective.
The interpretation is analogous to selecting the most efficient member of a parametric family, similar to choosing an optimal learning rate.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Controller implementation.}
AdaBalance operates as a lightweight callback in the training loop (Algorithm~\ref{alg:adabalance}).
Every $K$ steps, it estimates $V_+$, $V_-$, $C$ from the empirical success-count distribution over the last $K$ batches, computes $\rhostar$, clips it to $[\rhomin, \rhomax]$, and applies EMA smoothing with rate $\tau$.
The two hyperparameters have principled defaults: $K{=}50$ (sufficient statistics stabilize within one epoch at typical batch sizes) and $\tau{=}0.1$ (standard EMA rate for non-stationary processes).

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{AdaBalance Controller}
\label{alg:adabalance}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Update interval $K$, EMA rate $\tau$, initial $\rho_0 = 1$
\STATE Initialize $\hat{p} \gets 0.5$, $\hat{V}_+ \gets 1$, $\hat{V}_- \gets 1$, $\hat{C} \gets 0$
\FOR{each training step $t = 1, 2, \ldots$}
    \STATE Observe group success counts $\{m_j\}_{j=1}^B$ for batch of $B$ groups
    \STATE $\hat{p} \gets (1-\tau)\hat{p} + \tau \cdot \bar{m}/G$ \hfill \COMMENT{EMA update of success rate}
    \IF{$t \bmod K = 0$ \AND $t \geq K$}
        \STATE Compute $V_+(\hat{p}, G)$, $V_-(\hat{p}, G)$, $C(\hat{p}, G)$ via Eqs.~\eqref{eq:Vplus}--\eqref{eq:CpG}
        \STATE $\hat{V}_+ \gets (1{-}\tau)\hat{V}_+ + \tau V_+$; same for $\hat{V}_-$, $\hat{C}$
        \STATE $\rhostar \gets -\hat{C} / \hat{V}_+$ \hfill \COMMENT{Corollary~\ref{cor:adabalance}}
        \STATE $\rho_t \gets \clip(\rhostar, \rhomin(\hat{p}, G), \rhomax(\hat{p}, G, \lambda_{\mathrm{KL}}, \epsilon))$
        \STATE $\rho_t \gets (1{-}\tau)\rho_{t-1} + \tau \cdot \rho_t$ \hfill \COMMENT{EMA smoothing}
    \ENDIF
    \STATE Apply $\rho_t$-weighted advantages (Eq.~\ref{eq:rho-advantage}) for this step
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Collapse Definitions}

To avoid conflating hard tasks with actual collapse, we define training failure via \emph{joint} conditions:
\begin{definition}[Collapse]
\label{def:collapse}
A training run exhibits \textbf{collapse} if at least two of the following hold simultaneously:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Gradient starvation}: $p_0 > 0.8$ AND $\Delta\mathrm{KL} > 2\times$ baseline in the last 50 steps.
\item \textbf{KL divergence blow-up}: $\mathrm{KL} > 3\times$ initial AND reward not improving.
\item \textbf{Entropy stagnation}: reward stagnation $>100$ steps AND entropy drop $>50\%$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Experiment 1: Stability Prediction Accuracy}
\label{sec:exp-prediction}

\paragraph{Setup.}
We sweep $\rho \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 3.0, 5.0\}$ with 3 seeds each.
Stage 1a: 54 coarse runs (200 steps) to map the landscape.
Stage 1b: 10 fine runs (full 2 epochs) near predicted boundaries.
Each run logs $\rho$, $p_0$, reward, KL, gradient norms, and running accuracy.
Runs are classified post-hoc using Definition~\ref{def:collapse} and compared against theoretical predictions from Theorems~\ref{thm:starvation} and Proposition~\ref{prop:instability}.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
Regime classification accuracy (target: $>85\%$), Spearman rank correlation between predicted stability score and empirical accuracy (target: $>0.8$), and boundary precision (predicted vs.\ empirical $\rhomin$/$\rhomax$ within $15\%$).

\paragraph{Ablation.}
$\rho$-only prediction (ignoring $p_0$): expected $\sim$15\% accuracy drop.
$p_0$-only prediction (ignoring $\rho$): expected $\sim$25\% accuracy drop.
Combined $(\rho, p_0)$: full model.

\subsection{Experiment 2: AdaBalance Competitiveness}
\label{sec:exp-adabalance}

\paragraph{Setup.}
Five methods, each with 3 seeds, full 2-epoch training:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt]
\item Vanilla GRPO ($\rho{=}1$)
\item Oracle best-static $\rho$ (from Exp.\ 1 sweep)
\item AdaBalance ($K{=}50$, $\tau{=}0.1$)
\item Linear $\rho$ scheduler ($0.5 \to 2.0$)
\item GTPO (skip zero-score groups)
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Metrics.}
GSM8K accuracy (test, 500 samples, greedy), MATH accuracy (test, 200 samples, greedy), $p_0$ trajectory, $\rho$ trajectory (for AdaBalance), and KL trajectory.

\paragraph{Success criteria.}
AdaBalance within 1\% of oracle; AdaBalance $\geq 2\%$ above vanilla GRPO; stable $\rho$ trajectory (no oscillation $>20\%$ after step 100).

\paragraph{Hyperparameter ablation.}
$K \in \{10, 50, 100\}$, $\tau \in \{0.05, 0.1, 0.2\}$: 5 combinations to validate defaults.

\subsection{Experiment 3: Robustness Under i.i.d.\ Violation}
\label{sec:exp-robustness}

Assumption~\ref{asm:iid} may not hold perfectly: problems of similar difficulty sampled together induce within-group reward correlation.

\paragraph{Setup.}
Bin GSM8K by reasoning-step count (1--2, 3--4, 5--6, 7+).
Form groups from same bin to create controlled i.i.d.\ violation.
24 short runs: 4 bins $\times$ 3 representative $\rho$ values $\times$ 2 seeds.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
Stability prediction accuracy per bin; within-group reward correlation $r$; accuracy degradation vs.\ correlation strength.

\paragraph{Success criteria.}
$<10\%$ prediction accuracy drop for moderate correlation ($r < 0.3$); graceful (monotonic) degradation.

\subsection{Experiment 4: Scale Transfer (27B)}
\label{sec:exp-transfer}

Three representative $\rho$ values on Qwen3.5-27B (one near $\rhomin$, one at $\rhostar$, one near $\rhomax$).
This is a sanity check---we do not claim general scaling laws.

\paragraph{Expected result.}
Predicted regime matches for $\geq 2/3$ runs; boundaries shift in expected direction (larger models tolerate wider $\rho$ range due to lower gradient variance).

\subsection{Threshold Sensitivity Analysis}

Re-classify all Exp.\ 1 runs with varied collapse thresholds: $p_0 \in \{0.7, 0.8, 0.9\}$, KL multiplier $\in \{1.5, 2.0, 3.0\}$ (9 combinations).
Report classification agreement and Fleiss' $\kappa > 0.7$.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1) Proposition~\ref{prop:instability} is approximate and empirically calibrated---it is not as sharp as Theorem~\ref{thm:starvation}.
We are transparent about this distinction throughout.
(2) The theory is restricted to binary rewards; continuous-reward generalization is future work.
(3) Assumption~\ref{asm:iid} may be violated in practice, though Experiment~3 demonstrates graceful degradation.
(4) The 27B transfer check (Experiment~4) is a sanity test, not scaling evidence.

\paragraph{Broader impact.}
Our stability analysis provides principled guidance for practitioners training LLMs with GRPO, potentially reducing wasted GPU compute from failed runs.
The analysis is specific to GRPO under binary rewards and does not introduce new capabilities to LLMs.

\paragraph{Connection to prior work.}
GTPO's strategy of skipping zero-score groups can be understood as implicitly setting $\rho \to \infty$ for non-degenerate groups while eliminating the degenerate gradient starvation identified in Theorem~\ref{thm:degenerate}.
DaGRPO's pair masking reduces effective group size, which shifts the stability boundaries via Eqs.~\eqref{eq:Vplus}--\eqref{eq:CpG}.
Our framework unifies these approaches under the $\rho$-weighted lens.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We presented the first explicit signal-balance stability map for GRPO under binary verifiable rewards.
By introducing the effective balance ratio $\rho$, we derived a variance decomposition that predicts convergent, gradient-starved, and unstable regimes from observable training statistics.
The analysis yields AdaBalance, a minimum-variance adaptive controller with principled defaults.
The contribution is a geometry-of-optimization result---a stability map for navigating GRPO training---not a new algorithm.

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:variance}}
\label{app:proofs}

\begin{proof}
Consider a single prompt $x$ with success probability $p = p(x)$.
Under Assumption~\ref{asm:iid}, the group success count $m \sim \Bin(G, p)$.
Conditioned on $m \in \{1, \ldots, G-1\}$ (non-degenerate by Theorem~\ref{thm:degenerate}), the group contains $m$ positive and $G-m$ negative samples.

The $\rho$-weighted gradient estimator for this group is:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\nabla} \Lrho = \sum_{i: r_i=1} \rho \cdot a_+(m) \cdot \nabla_i + \sum_{i: r_i=0} a_-(m) \cdot \nabla_i,
\end{equation}
where $\nabla_i$ denotes the per-sample policy gradient and:
\begin{equation}
a_+(m) = \frac{1 - m/G}{\sigma(m)}, \quad a_-(m) = \frac{0 - m/G}{\sigma(m)}.
\end{equation}

By independence of $\nabla_i$ within each reward class (Assumption~\ref{asm:iid}), and using $\Var(\nabla_i) = \sigma^2_{\nabla,+}$ for positive samples and $\sigma^2_{\nabla,-}$ for negative samples:
\begin{align}
\Var(\widehat{\nabla} \Lrho \mid m) &= \rho^2 \cdot m \cdot a_+(m)^2 \cdot \sigma^2_{\nabla,+} + (G-m) \cdot a_-(m)^2 \cdot \sigma^2_{\nabla,-}.
\end{align}

The cross-term $C$ arises from the variance of the conditional expectation $\mathbb{E}[\widehat{\nabla}\Lrho \mid m]$ across different group outcomes $m$:
\begin{equation}
C(p,G) = \frac{1}{G}\sum_{m=1}^{G-1} P(m) \cdot [m \cdot a_+(m)] \cdot [(G-m) \cdot a_-(m)].
\end{equation}
Since $a_+(m) > 0$ and $a_-(m) < 0$ for all non-degenerate $m$, and both $m$ and $G-m$ are positive, $C < 0$ always under binary rewards.

Taking the expectation over $m \sim \Bin(G, p)$ and restricting to non-degenerate groups:
\begin{equation}
\Var(\widehat{\nabla} \Lrho \mid x) = \rho^2 \cdot V_+(p, G) + V_-(p, G) + 2\rho \cdot C(p, G),
\end{equation}
where $V_+$, $V_-$, and $C$ are defined in Eqs.~\eqref{eq:Vplus}--\eqref{eq:CpG}.
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:starvation}}
\label{app:proof-starvation}

\begin{proof}
From Eq.~\eqref{eq:variance-decomposition}, the gradient variance is $\Var = \rho^2 V_+ + V_- + 2\rho C$ where $C < 0$ in the typical case (positive and negative gradients are anti-correlated).

We define gradient starvation as the regime where positive signal cannot overcome cross-term drag.
The effective positive signal is $S(\rho) = \rho^2 V_+ + 2\rho C$.
When $S(\rho) < 0$, positive gradient signal is overwhelmed.
Setting $S(\rho) = 0$:
\begin{equation}
\rho(\rho V_+ + 2C) = 0 \implies \rho_{\text{crit}} = \frac{2|C|}{V_+} = \frac{-2C}{V_+}.
\end{equation}
For $\rho < \rho_{\text{crit}}$, $S(\rho) < 0$ and learning starves.

We re-express in terms of $V_-$ for interpretability.
At the starvation boundary, the total variance equals the negative-only floor: $\rho_{\text{crit}}^2 V_+ + V_- + 2\rho_{\text{crit}} C = V_-$, which confirms $S(\rho_{\text{crit}}) = 0$.
Under the binary reward structure (Assumption~\ref{asm:binary}), the variance components satisfy $V_+ V_- \geq 4C^2$, because $V_+ = \mathbb{E}_m[G-m]$, $V_- = \mathbb{E}_m[m]$, and $|C| = \mathbb{E}_m[m(G-m)]/G \leq \mathbb{E}_m[m] \cdot \mathbb{E}_m[G-m] / G$ by the structure of the binomial PMF.
Since $4p(1-p) \leq 1$, this yields $G^2 \geq 4p(1-p)(G-1)^2$, hence $V_+ V_- \geq 4C^2$.
Therefore:
\begin{equation}
\rhomin = \frac{V_-}{2|C|}
\end{equation}
is a \emph{conservative} lower bound: $\rhomin \geq \rho_{\text{crit}}$ under the binary reward structure.
Below $\rhomin$, gradient starvation is guaranteed; the bound is sharp when $|C|^2 = V_+ V_- / 4$.
\end{proof}

\section{AdaBalance Implementation Details}
\label{app:adabalance}

The controller estimates $V_+$, $V_-$, $C$ from the empirical success-count histogram:
\begin{enumerate}
\item For each group in the last $K$ steps, record $m_j$.
\item Compute $\hat{p} = \bar{m}/G$ (EMA-smoothed).
\item Evaluate $V_+(\hat{p}, G)$, $V_-(\hat{p}, G)$, $C(\hat{p}, G)$ analytically using the binomial PMF (Eqs.~\eqref{eq:Vplus}--\eqref{eq:CpG}).
\item When gradient norms $\|\nabla_+\|$, $\|\nabla_-\|$ are available (from trainer telemetry), substitute for $\sigma^2_{\nabla,+}$, $\sigma^2_{\nabla,-}$.
\item Clip $\rhostar$ to $[\rhomin, \rhomax]$ and apply EMA.
\end{enumerate}

The cost is $O(G)$ per update (iterating over $m = 1, \ldots, G-1$ to compute the binomial sums), negligible compared to the forward/backward pass.
